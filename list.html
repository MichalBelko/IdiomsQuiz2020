List of all idioms used in our quiz

A dog's life
A very unhappy and unpleasant life

"Mark's been leading a dog's life since he was fired from the job."

Add insult to injury
To make an argument or bad situation worse

"The discovery that the government was aware of the cover-up has really added insult to injury."

Another/a second bite of the cherry
Another opportunity to do something

"He missed a medal in the 100 metres, but will get a second bite of the cherry in the 400 metres."

At a rate of knots
If someone does something at a rate of knots, they do it very quickly

"She got through her work at a rate of knots."

Be at sea
To be very confused

"I'm completely at sea with the new computer system."

Be at peace
A gentle way of saying that someone is dead

"Now she is at peace and her suffering is over."

Be on the cards
To be likely to happen

"So you think they'll get married next year?" "I think it's in the cards."

Be on cloud nine
To be extremely happy and excited

"Was Helen pleased about getting that job?" "Pleased? She was on cloud nine!"

Be knee-high to a grasshopper
To be very small or young

"I've lived here since I was knee-high to a grasshopper."

Be pushing up (the) daisies
To be dead

"I'll be pushing up the daisies long before it happens."

Be of unsound mind
To be mentally ill

"Nevertheless, this man was of unsound mind."

Be the bee's knees
To be excellent or of an extremely high standard

"Have you tried this ice cream? It's the bee's knees, it really is."

Bite the dust
To die

"I think our car just bit the dust."

Blessing in disguise
Something that seems bad or unlucky at first but causes something good to happen later

"Being laid off was a blessing in disguise – within a month I got a much better job."

Bone idle
Extremely lazy

"He never does any exercise - he's bone idle."

Burn the midnight oil
To work late into the night

"It is very very hard assignment, and I had to burn the midnight oil to get it completed on time."

Burn the candle at both ends
To work or do other things from early in the morning until late at night and so get very little rest

"I'm burning the candle at both ends to try and finish my assignment for tomorrow."

Buy the farm
To die

"If you crash at 60 mph, the odds are you'll buy the farm."

Call of nature
The need to use the toilet

"I'm afraid I have to answer a call of nature."

Call a spade a spade
To say the truth about something, even if it is not polite or pleasant

"That dress made her look fat, let's call a spade a spade before she goes out wearing it and embarrasses herself."

Call it a day
To stop what you are doing because you do not want to do any more or think you have done enough

"I'm getting a bit tired now - let's call it a day."

Catch/cop/get some z's
To sleep

"All I want to do is go home and catch some z's."

Chew the fat
To talk with someone in an informal and friendly way

"We sat in a bar most of the evening just chewing the fat."

Down the road
In the future

"Lots of things will have changed a few years down the road."

Every cloud has a silver lining
Said to emphasize that every difficult or unpleasant situation has some advantage

"I found a new job after all and I like this one much better than the last!”.. “You see, every cloud has a silver lining."

Feel blue
To feel sad or depressed

"I have been feeling blue ever since I have heard that my daughter will be moving abroad for good."

Fender bender
Road accident in which the vehicles involved are only slightly damaged

"I hope my insurance company isn't going to charge me for that little fender bender."

Get the axe
To lose a job

"Religious programmes will be the first to get the axe if she's put in charge of the station."

Haul someone over the coals
To criticize someone severely for something they have done

"Ministers were hauled over the coals by the select committee for failing to disclose vital information in the inquiry."

Have a long face
If you have a long face, you look sad

"Why do you have such a long face?" "My boyfriend doesn't want to see me any more."

Hit the bottle
To start to drink too much alcohol

"She managed to resist alcohol for a year, then hit the bottle again when her husband died."

Hit the ceiling
To become extremely angry

"Dad will hit the roof when he finds out I dented the car."

Hit the sack
To go sleep

"I have a busy day tomorrow, so I think I'll hit the sack."

In the dumps
Not successful

"The team has been in the dumps all season."

Jam tomorrow
Something good that is promised but never happens

"As children we were always being promised jam tomorrow, if only we would be patient."

Line sb's pockets
To make someone richer, especially in illegal or dishonest ways

"They're the sort of Washington politicians who would line the pockets of their Wall Street friends."

Live in clover
To enjoy a life of money and comfort

"With the income from the family estate, she's in clover."

Man of means
Rich man

"What made you the man of means?"

Mad as a hatter
Extremely silly or stupid

"The way he dressed for the event, he must be as mad as a hatter."

Make headway
To make progress or get closer to achieving something

"I'm trying to learn to drive, but I'm not making much headway"

Meet your maker
To die

"That is going to be rather difficult. She met her maker ten years ago."

On the wagon
If you are on the wagon, you have decided not to drink any alcohol

"He was on the wagon for ten years, when he was living in Connecticut."

Pie in the sky
Something that you hope will happen but is very unlikely to happen

"Their plans to set up their own business are just pie in the sky."

Pull your socks up
To make an effort to improve your work or behaviour because it is not good enough

"He's going to have to pull his socks up if he wants to stay in the team."

Pull sb's leg
To try to persuade someone to believe something that is not true, as a joke

"Is it really your car or are you pulling my leg?"

Say uncle
To admit failure

"Even Richard Nixon had to say uncle eventually."

See eye to eye
If two people see eye to eye, they agree with each other

"My sisters don't see eye to eye with me about the arrangements."

Shoot the breeze
To spend time talking about things that are not important

"We sat out on the porch, just shooting the breeze."

Sick at heart
very unhappy

"David was sick at heart about having to leave his family behind."

Spill the beans
To tell people secret information

"So who spilled the beans about her affair with David?"

Split hairs
To argue about small details of something

"I'm sorry to split hairs, but your portion of the bill is $25.97, not $25.79."

Step on the gas
To drive faster

"You've got to hit the gas when you're merging onto a motorway."

Storm in a teacup
A lot of unnecessary anger and worry about a matter that is not important

"It seems to me to be a storm in a teacup about these business letters."

Take a battering
To be defeated heavily

"Once again, our team took a battering on Saturday."

Take the mickey(out of someone)
To laugh at someone and make them seem silly, in a funny or unkind way

"A group of other boys were taking the mickey out of him."

Talk turkey
To discuss something in a direct way without avoiding difficult issues

"Few politicians are willing to talk turkey about immigration."

Talk through your hat
Talk nonsense

"Nothing of what he said made sense - he was talking through his hat."

Throw in the towel
To admit a defeat or to give up

"She was ready to throw in the towel, but remembered her goal and kept going"

Throw caution to the wind
To decide not to worry about possible dangers or risks

"Faced with one of the worst financial crises in recent memory, British investors threw caution to the wind and scrambled to buy stocks."

Tie the knot
To get married

"They have been dating each other for quite some time now and are planning to tie the knot."

To spin a yarn
To tell a story

"They spun us a yarn about being in desperate need of money."

When hell freezes over
If you say that something will happen when hell freezes over, you mean that it will never happen.

"I will forgive Ben when hell freezes over."

Work your fingers to the bone
To work extremely hard, especially for a long time

"She worked her fingers to the bone to provide a home and food for seven children."

- An idiom used mainly in the USA
- An informal idiom
